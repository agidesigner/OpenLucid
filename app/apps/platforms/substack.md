Platform writing rules (Substack newsletter / long-form email):

**Purpose and voice**: A newsletter is **not** a blog. A blog sits on a website waiting to be found; a newsletter lands in someone's inbox next to email from their mother and their boss. That context demands:
- **A real person writing** — first person, a consistent voice, strong narrator identity. Institutional "we" kills newsletters.
- **Serialization** — readers subscribe because they like YOU. Each issue reinforces the relationship, not just delivers information.
- **Earned attention** — the reader gave you inbox real estate. Don't waste it with filler intro.

**Subject line + subtitle (the craft pair)**:

Subject and subtitle are **two distinct instruments**. Top newsletters (Lenny, Packy McCormick, Platformer, Dan Shipper) treat the subtitle as a first-class element, not a preview-text afterthought.

- **Subject**: ~50 chars median, cap ~70. The **claim or promise**.
- **Subtitle (dek)**: 90-160 chars. Adds the curious detail, twist, consequence, or counter-question — **it completes the headline, doesn't restate it**. Substack renders it on-page in larger type under the H1; email clients use it as preview text.

Examples of strong headline + subtitle pairs:
- Lenny: "Not all AI agents are created equal" → "A framework for categorizing and prioritizing your agent initiatives"
- Platformer: "Sam Altman's second thoughts" → "OpenAI's CEO is asking the public to lower the temperature on AI. But who turned it up in the first place?"
- Every: "The Folder Is the Agent" → "I'm running 44 AI agents across multiple projects. Each one is just a model pointed at a folder."

**Multi-story briefing format** (for newsletters covering multiple items per issue, like Every or Weekly Dose of Optimism): use a two-deck subtitle — the main story plus "Plus: X, Y, Z" teasing secondary items.

**Subject archetypes that work**:
- Number + specific outcome: "7 pricing mistakes that cost us $200k"
- Behind-the-scenes: "What happened when I fired my biggest client"
- Contrarian take: "Stop using Stripe Atlas"
- **Named framework** (~25% of top subjects — the strongest archetype): "The Waterline Model", "Bad Analogies", "The Poison Arrow Principle"
- Direct question: "Is your homepage doing this?"
- **Numbered series** (only when the series IS the brand, e.g. "Weekly Dose of Optimism #189"): acceptable. Random "Issue #47" on a personal newsletter is still dead weight.

**Subject archetypes to avoid**:
- Generic "Issue #N" numbering when the series has no standalone identity
- ALLCAPS (spam triggers)
- Vague "Some thoughts on pricing"

**Opening line** (first line of body, cold unless you have an established greeting ritual):

~75% of top issues skip greetings entirely. "Hi friends 👋" works as a **consistent brand ritual** (Packy McCormick's Not Boring uses it every issue) but never as a filler default.

Recurring opener formulas that land across the top tier:
- **"Over the past N [months/year], I've had the same conversation [N times]"** (Lenny)
- **"There's a moment most [role] recognize…"** (Lenny, Waterline)
- **"I spent N months trying to…"** (Dan Shipper, Folder Is the Agent)
- **Flat declarative**: "[X] secretly runs our world" (Packy, Electromagnetism)
- **Contrarian meta**: "I wasn't planning on sending an essay this week, but…" (Packy, Bad Analogies)
- **Date stamp**: "July 2024." followed by a specific fact.

Lead with the sharpest version of your thesis in the first 3-5 sentences — name the problem, quote a surprising stat, or state the claim you'll defend.

**Guest / co-written framing**: "Co-written with X" or a meta-intro paragraph introducing a guest essayist is a common top-newsletter pattern (Not Boring runs this on Electromagnetism, World Models; Lenny frames Michelle Rial's guest illustrations). If the LLM is writing a guest-authored-style piece, open with a 2-3 sentence intro from the host voice, then hand off.

**Body structure** (standard issue 2,000-3,500 words; short 800-1,500; deep-dive 5,000-10,000 only with original reporting or a genuine framework):

- **Lead** (first 3-5 sentences): hook into the promise. Name the problem. Quote a surprising stat or claim.
- **Thesis paragraph**: state what this issue will give the reader.
- **Meat** (70% of the word count): deliver. Use H2 subheadings every 200-400 words (top issues run 5-12 H2s). Bullets / numbered lists for scannable segments, prose for narrative segments. Mix both.
- **Pull quotes / block quotes**: 1-3 per long post; they anchor skimmers and give shareable moments.
- **Personal disclosure**: at least one honest, specific story/number/admission per issue. The reason people subscribe to newsletters vs. reading blogs is they want YOU, not a polished essay.
- **Closing**: not a summary — a closing thought that lands. Often a single sentence, italicized or set off.

**Sponsor / ad placement** (if the newsletter runs sponsors): Place the ad block **at the top** as the first H2 block ("Today's [newsletter] is brought to you by…"), before the essay starts. Never paywall sponsor reads; never bury the sponsor mid-essay. Top-of-post is ~30% of paid newsletter issues. This is counterintuitive but strongly established.

**Paid-tier pitch** (for writers with paid tiers): default is a **paywall cut at ~30-50% into the post** — after the thesis and initial value, before the deepest analysis. Avoid mid-post "Upgrade for $$" banners; avoid pitching paid in the opening. The paywall itself is the pitch.

**CTAs** (pick 1-2, not all):
- **Subscribe** (primary for free issues reaching new readers): "If you liked this, subscribe for a weekly [topic]."
- **Paywall cut** (on paid-enabled issues): the cut itself.
- **Reply** (rare, ~15% of top issues): "Hit reply and tell me [specific question]." Newsletter's unique advantage over blog — underused.
- **Share** (less common in top tier than our early guidance assumed): works best for personal-story issues, not frameworks.

**P.S.** (~60% of top issues use one — more common than we previously listed):
- Podcast / audio-version link ("If you prefer, you can listen to this post in podcast form") — Lenny does this consistently
- Preview of next issue ("Next week: X")
- Soft product plug or bundle mention
- "What I'm reading / listening to / watching" coda
- Pick ONE. Multiple P.S.s feel cluttered.

**Formatting**:
- Short paragraphs (1-4 sentences). Email readers scan.
- Headers (H2) every few paragraphs for scannability.
- Images: 2-4 per long post common; hero image at top in ~85% of essays. News-style briefings (Platformer) often skip images entirely — image use is genre-dependent, not universal.
- Links: inline. Most references; don't hide referrals.
- **Bold AND italic both work** in modern Substack rendering. Use **bold** for keyword-scanning in long prose so skimmers can find anchors; *italic* for tone and emphasis.

**Footnotes**: acceptable for asides in 3,000+ word deep-dives (Not Boring Electromagnetism has 6; The Diff uses them liberally). Keep **inline links** for references; use numbered end-of-post footnotes only for tangential context or sourcing.

**Length guidance**:
- **Short issue** (800-1,500 words): single insight, fast read. Good for weekly cadence.
- **Standard issue** (2,000-3,500 words): most top newsletters' sweet spot. This is longer than the '1,000-1,500' figure often cited.
- **Deep-dive** (5,000-10,000 words): monthly cornerstone piece. Justify with original data, detailed frameworks, or multi-case analysis.
- **Over 10,000**: only for annual reports or major case studies. Most subscribers won't finish.

**Anti-patterns**:
- "Hi friends, hope you're having a great week!" as filler (use it as brand ritual only, not default)
- Week-by-week personal updates that aren't tied to a promise
- Long wind-up before the actual content ("So this week I was thinking about...")
- Cross-promoting 5 other newsletters in every issue (subscriber fatigue)
- No personal stake — just a linkdump or summary of industry news without your angle
- Signup CTA every 3 paragraphs (makes readers feel hunted)
- Mid-essay ad placement (sponsor goes at top, never the middle)
- Inline "Upgrade for $$" banners as paid pitch (paywall cut is the modern pitch)

**Output format**: Section 1 is subject line + subtitle (in metadata). Section 2 is the opening (cold unless this newsletter has an established greeting). Middle sections carry H2 subheaders. Final section is the closing thought + one CTA + optional P.S.
